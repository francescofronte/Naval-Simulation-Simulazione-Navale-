# Naval Simulation (Simulazione Navale)
Modular C project simulating the transit of several cargo ships between harbours. Developed as a solution to the assignment of the Operating Systems course (2022/2023)

# Run the project
Download all files locally in a folder on your machine (a Linux OS is required). Open the shell in the folder and run 'make' to compile the code using the makefile. Then run: ./simulazioneNavale\
If you want to change the configuration parameters, edit their values in the file "configurazione.txt".

## Project Report
In the following report, the design choices made during the development of the “Naval Simulation” application for the final project of the Operating Systems Course, academic year 2022/2023, are summarized.

The software was designed, developed and tested between November 2022 and January 2023.

## Master

The Master process is responsible for preparing the initialization of the program, reading the configuration parameters from file and allocating appropriate shared memory areas for the sharing of initialization data and the collection of data for state dumps and simulation termination dumps, possibly protected by semaphores. The input parameters are subject to validation and reset to default values if the entered values could cause errors.

Subsequently, the Master generates through the fork() system call the child processes that will take on the roles of Porto, Nave and Meteo. The processes are started through the execv() system call.

The simulation begins only after all processes have completed their initialization phase. This is guaranteed by the use of an appropriate semaphore initialized to the number of processes involved.

During the simulation, the Master process keeps track of the elapsed days, synchronizes the processes through the sending of signals (SIGUSR1 is used for this purpose) and prints the daily reports, divided into sections relating to goods, ships and ports.

At the end of the simulation, which may occur upon expiration of the days or upon termination of all Nave processes (not upon exhaustion of demand or supply for the reasons illustrated later), the Master process prints the final dump, terminates the still active processes, removes the IPC structures and terminates. The termination phase is also synchronized through a semaphore.

## Merce

A lot of goods is defined in the program as a structure equipped with the fields “type”, “quantity”, “expiration”, “state flag” and “next”, which completely define its characteristics and allow its organization into linked lists. For each type of goods, quantity and duration before expiration are randomly extracted by the Master process during the initialization phase. When a lot of goods x is created at a port, it is assigned an expiration date equal to (current day) + (duration of goods x).

## Porto

The Porto process is first of all responsible for generating lots of some goods randomly selected at the beginning of each day. The ports forked with even and odd indices are selected alternately each day to produce the goods in the quantity (SO_FILL/SO_DAYS)/porti_che_producono. During the simulation, the quantity of goods produced approximates the SO_FILL parameter by truncation, with precision varying according to the input parameters (number of ships and ports, lot size, loading/unloading speed, meteorological parameters). The SO_FILL quantity, if reached, can only be reached on the last day of simulation. For this reason it is impossible to exhaust demand or supply before at least SO_DAYS days have elapsed. Each port generates and requests half of the available types of goods. Every good not generated by the port is requested by the port in quantity (SO_FILL/SO_PORTI)/(SO_MERCI/2). This criterion is slightly different in the case of a single good.

The port organizes its warehouse as a local array of structs. The generated and offered goods are arranged in alternating positions in the array. Each structure has the task of keeping track of present, shipped and received goods, signaling whether the i-th good is in demand or supply, and maintaining two pointers to the head and tail of a linked list where the lots of goods are stored. In an attempt to minimize waste of goods and improve program efficiency, lots of goods are always removed from the head and inserted at the tail of the list. At the beginning of each day, the port is also responsible for deallocating expired lots of goods and counting them as such, by examining the head of each linked list.

In addition to the warehouse array, each port has a shared support array, in whose cells the quantity of the i-th good requested by the port is written. The ships use this array to determine which goods should eventually be sent to the port during the unloading phase. Communication between ships and ports is managed through a message queue allocated by each port during the initialization phase. The messages exchanged between ships and ports consist of structures within which the characteristics of the lots of goods to be transferred are written.

## Nave

The Nave process is the entity most active in decisions and negotiations regarding the exchange of goods, unlike the port which merely generates the lots of goods and “puts on display” its demand and supply. The ship first decides whether to load or unload goods by comparing the ratio merce_a_bordo/SO_CAPACITY with a parameterized threshold SO_THRESHOLD varying from 0.1 to 0.9 (default: 0.5), then randomly selects a port to travel to and sleeps for the time necessary to reach it. Upon reaching the port, in the case of loading, the ship sends the port a message containing the quantity of goods it intends to load on board (given by capacity – merce_a_bordo). The port responds by sending messages containing information about the transferred lots. When it has exhausted its availability of goods or satisfied the ship’s request, the port sends a message with the goods type set to -1. At this point the ship disconnects from the port and resumes its execution cycle. In the case of unloading, instead, the ship directly sends the port messages containing data about the goods to be transferred according to the port’s requests, and disconnects once the availability on board has been exhausted or the port’s requests have been satisfied. When goods are transferred from a ship to a port, the lots are simply deallocated from the ship and counted as received by the port. In any case, the ship then sleeps for the time required to transfer the goods according to SO_LOADSPEED.

The ship allocates its goods in an array whose entries are heads of linked lists of the i-th good, managed similarly to the port warehouse. The ship also, at the beginning of each day, deallocates expired goods and counts them as such.

## Meteo

The Meteo process is responsible for managing various meteorological inconveniences that may disturb the continuation of the simulation. To do so it makes use of the SIGUSR2 signals (sent to ships and ports to signal storm and storm surge) and SIGTERM (sent to ships to signal the maelstrom). SIGUSR2 is sent at the beginning of each day, when the expiration of the previous day is signaled by the Master process. SIGTERM, on the other hand, is sent whenever the time indicated by the SO_MAELSTROM parameter has elapsed. The elapsed time is calculated based on the elapsed clock cycles, with the aid of the clock() function.

It should be noted that some operations, within ports and ships, are protected by signal masks, therefore it is not guaranteed that storm and storm surge will be handled instantaneously by the processes concerned. Termination, instead, is always immediate because it is handled by a handler and SIGTERM is not masked.

In the event of the termination of all ships, the Meteo process terminates itself. The Master process notices the termination of all ships during the printing of the next report, therefore it proceeds to print the final dump and terminate the simulation.

## Auxiliary Modules

The software includes two additional modules: “functions.c”, which contains the bodies of the functions used for the management of binary semaphores (taken from the slides of Course A), and “myheader.h”, a header file containing the declarations of the structures used in the application and the functions contained in the module mentioned above.

## Development and testing

The software was developed on an Ubuntu LTS 64-bit virtual machine (version 22.04.1) and tested on Ubuntu virtual machines and native Kali Linux 64-bit and Arch Linux 64-bit machines.

## Glossary

* Porto = Port
* Nave = Ship
* Meteo = Weather
* Merce = Goods
* porti_che_producono = ports_that_produce
* merce_a_bordo = goods_on_board
* SO_PORTI = number of ports
* SO_MERCI = number of goods types
* Maelstrom = large destructive whirlpool event used by the simulation
* functions.c = auxiliary source file containing semaphore-management functions
* myheader.h = application header file
